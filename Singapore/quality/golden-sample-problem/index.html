Have you ever experienced a situation in new product development where the prototype was perfect, but the moment mass production started, the defect rate skyrocketed?

The best way to prevent this is the approach practiced by Komatsu Bane: "Use mass production machines from the prototyping stage."

Generally, prototypes are often made by skilled craftsmen using manual tools or specialized prototyping machines. This often creates the "Golden Sample"‚Äîa perfect specimen that cannot be reproduced at scale. However, we do things differently. From the very first prototype, we use the actual automatic coiling machines and equipment intended for mass production to establish manufacturing conditions and build in quality. This means that if the prototype gets the green light, we can transition to mass production smoothly and reliably.

Read this article to understand why this method drastically reduces development lead time and cuts costs!

Why Does "Mass Production" Fail Even When the "Prototype is Perfect"? ü§î

When outsourcing to suppliers, you might feel relieved receiving a high-quality prototype (Golden Sample), only to find that the delivered mass-produced items vary wildly in quality and are unusable. This "wall between prototype and mass production" is a serious challenge plaguing many procurement managers.

The Limits of Hand-Made Prototypes and Lack of "Reproducibility"

When making prototypes, many manufacturers prioritize "just giving it shape," often using handmade methods or simple jigs dedicated to prototyping. A prototype created by a skilled craftsman through repeated manual adjustments will certainly perform according to specs. However, that story applies only to "that single piece."

The moment you try to produce the same thing on an automatic machine running at high speed for mass production, those subtle manual adjustments become impossible to replicate, leading to frequent issues like tolerance deviations and breakage. This is the typical failure pattern caused by a non-reproducible "Golden Sample."

Waste of Cost and Time Caused by "Redesign" at Mass Production

If the prototyping machine and the mass production machine are different, you have to restart the "condition setting for mass production" from scratch right before the launch. In some cases, the design drawings themselves must be modified to suit the mass production machine (rework).

This causes development delays ranging from weeks to months and incurs additional costs for tool modifications, becoming a major factor in missed business opportunities. Komatsu Bane's approach is a strategy designed to fundamentally eliminate these "hidden costs."

Komatsu Bane's Answer: Make it with "Mass Production Machines" from the Start! üéØ

We believe that "prototyping is not a rehearsal for mass production; it is mass production itself." This philosophy is the reason why we continue to be chosen by precision equipment manufacturers around the world.

The Reason for Strictly Adhering to "Mass Production Conditions" from the Prototype Stage

At Komatsu Bane, even for a single prototype, we use the automatic forming machines (such as coiling machines) scheduled for future mass production.

By conducting prototypes using the same machines, the same jigs, and the same programs as in mass production, we can identify all potential "problems that could occur during mass production" at that stage. Since mass production validation is completed at the prototype stage, we can immediately start full production as soon as the customer gives the GO sign. This is an extremely powerful competitive advantage in modern business, where speed is critical.

Flexible Production System Supported by Over 600 Machines and Skilled Workers

The background that enables this method is our overwhelming equipment capacity. We have approximately 600 diverse machines in operation, ranging from the latest NC (Numerical Control) spring forming machines to mechanical forming machines specialized for specific processing.

Furthermore, skilled technicians who have mastered these machines design and manufacture unique jigs (tools) in-house. We have a system where decisions like "For this spring shape, that specific machine is best" are made instantly, and the optimal equipment is used consistently from prototype to mass production.

ISO Certification and Thorough Data Management

Komatsu Bane has obtained ISO9001 and "Eco Action 21" (Environmental Management System formulated by the Ministry of the Environment) certifications and maintains a strict quality management system.
